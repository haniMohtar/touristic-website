Temple of Jupiter

About the Monument

The Temple of Jupiter, located in the Heliopolis of Baalbek, Lebanon, was one of the largest and most ornate temples in the entire Roman Empire. It was dedicated to the Roman god Jupiter (Zeus). The temple stands on a massive platform known as the "Trilithon," which contains three of the largest stones ever moved by humans in antiquity, each weighing approximately 800 tons. Today, six of the original 54 massive Corinthian columns still stand, serving as an iconic symbol of Lebanon's rich archaeological heritage.

History

Construction began in the mid-1st century AD and took several decades to complete. The temple was part of a grand complex that included the Temple of Bacchus and the Temple of Venus. The columns are nearly 20 meters high and over 2 meters in diameter, carved from single blocks of Aswan granite or local limestone. The site reflects the fusion of Roman architectural grandeur with local Semitic religious traditions. Despite centuries of earthquakes and stone recycling, the ruins remain one of the best-preserved and most visited Roman sites in the world.

Quick Facts

  • Location: Baalbek, Beqaa Valley, Lebanon
  • Built: 1st Century AD
  • UNESCO Site: Designated in 1984
  • Height: Columns reach approx. 20 meters
  • Unique Feature: The "Trilithon" stones are among the heaviest monoliths in history.
