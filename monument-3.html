Sun Temple of Konark

About the Monument

The Sun Temple of Konark is a 13th-century Hindu temple dedicated to the Sun God, Surya. Located on the coastline of Odisha, India, it is designed as a colossal chariot with 24 massive stone wheels, pulled by seven horses. The temple is a UNESCO World Heritage site and is celebrated for its intricate stone carvings that cover every inch of the structure, depicting everything from daily life and mythical creatures to complex geometric patterns.

History

Built around 1250 AD by King Narasimhadeva I of the Eastern Ganga Dynasty, the temple was originally constructed at the mouth of the Chandrabhaga River. The 24 wheels are not just decorative; they are accurately carved sundials that can be used to calculate the time to the minute. Though much of the main sanctuary has collapsed over the centuries, the remaining audience hall (Jagamohana) still stands as a testament to the advanced engineering and artistic brilliance of ancient India.

Quick Facts

  • Location: Konark, Odisha, India
  • Built: ~1250 AD
  • Architectural Style: Kalinga Architecture
  • Entrance Fee: Paid
  • Unique Feature: The temple's 24 wheels represent the 24 hours of a day.
